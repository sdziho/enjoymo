Old Bridge

Old Bridge is a famous bridge over the river Neretva in Mostar. It was built between 1557 - 1566, the work of the Turkish builder Hajrudin. After the reconstruction in 2004, the bridge was included in the UNESCO World Heritage List. It is known as the main symbol of Mostar and Herzegovina. Mostar's Old Bridge is a stone bridge built of tenelija stone. The arch is almost 29 meters long and 20 meters high, bent near a lowered semicircle. It is characterized by a thin and elegant shape: the profile of the bridge was so thin and high above the water that it was difficult for many to imagine that such a building could be made of huge stone blocks. The bridge is a bright bright color that changes during the day, depending on the sun.

Biščevića house

Biščevića house, better known as Biščevića corner, is located on the east bank of the Neretva River in Biščevića Street. This old Ottoman house dates from the 17th century. The corner protruding above the Neretva on two very high and boldly placed stone pillars, presents this house as the boldest composed work of residential architecture from the Turkish period in Mostar. The chamber or divanhana has preserved the original Ottoman style, the house abounds in authentic furniture, and the garden is a representative example of the Turkish style of construction. Entrance to the house is 3 KM.

Muslibegovića house

The National Monument "Muslibegović House" located near the Karađoz-beg Mosque is one of the most monumental monuments of urban housing architecture of the Ottoman period in Bosnia and Herzegovina. The complex consisting of a family house (women's courtyard - haremluk) and a business part (men's courtyard - selamluk) was built in the early 18th century, and the main residential building was reconstructed and extended in 1872 on the model of representative buildings in Istanbul. Unlike residential buildings from the earlier period, it was built in the central system of room layout and has 4 floors. The entrance to the residential part is two-arched with a central pillar in the middle, which reflects the influence of the Mediterranean. The building has preserved its authentic form and woodcut, as well as original objects, writings and exhibits, provides visitors with an insight into the way of life of a rich bey family in past centuries. In addition to visiting and seeing the museum setting, visitors are offered the opportunity to consume traditional drinks and cakes and one part of the complex also offers the opportunity to sleep in the original setting.

Crooked Bridge

The bridge was built by the Turkish architect Ceyvan Ketoda in 1558. It is believed that this bridge was built as a test bridge before the construction of the Old Bridge. The old bridge was completed in 1566, when it acquired the title of the greatest architectural achievement built during the Turkish rule in the Balkans. No matter how many times you have crossed the Old Bridge, every crossing is an exciting experience. This one-arched stone bridge was built on the model of the original bridge that towered over the Neretva four hundred years ago, and which was built according to the project of Hajrudin, a student of one of the greatest Turkish builders, Sinan. The length of the vault is 28.7 meters, or 21 m above the level of the Neretva River. Two towers, Halebija and Tara, were built for the guards of the bridge, which served as a weapons warehouse during the Turkish rule

Old bazar

In the center of the Old Town you can enjoy the oldest zone of Mostar - Kujundžiluk (old bazaar), a gracefully paved alley from the middle of the 16th century, which still houses characteristic craft shops and traditional restaurants. In the past, it was the lifeblood of trade throughout the region, and during the Ottoman Empire it numbered more than 500 stores. Despite the changes that come with time, this street has retained its authentic ion zionomy and is characterized by mosques and small shops, and to this day it has preserved some of the characteristic crafts, such as hand-made copper and carpet weaving. Along with the Old Bridge, the main character in the story of the whole neighborhood, the old bazaar is a suggestive cross-section of architecture and, at the same time, everyday life in Mostar, represented by liveliness in shops, restaurants (where you can enjoy excellent traditional cuisine) and crowded houses.

Neolithic times

Witness of the existence of Neolithic man is the prehistoric cave settlement "Zelena pećina" in Blagaj. It is about 10 km southeast of the city of Mostar, in an almost inaccessible location, above the source of the river Buna. To the west of the cave, on the very slope, lie the remains of the medieval town of Blagaj.

The green cave faces the entrance to the west, divided by one living rock into two parts. Access to the entrance to the cave was very difficult, very steep, and from the top completely inaccessible, so the cave was an ideal shelter for Neolithic man from unwanted visitors and atmospheres. In the cave itself there was a larger boulder next to which Neolithic man burned and had an open hearth. The chronological dating of the Green Cave, like other sites of the Early Neolithic, has many uncertainties, but, according to some C14 tests, this Neolithic site dates from the 5th millennium BC (approximately 4200 BC). Ceramic vessels excavated in the Green Cave are rough and monochrome polished vessels: they are conical vessels, belly and cylindrical vases with various ornamental motifs, as follows: - impressions of fingers and nails during production ) - motifs of classes - motifs of a wicker basket or motifs made of some kind of shell.

Roman times

Mostar and its surroundings represent a geographical framework that stretches from Drežnica in the north, across Nevesinjsko polje in the east and the eastern borders of the municipalities of Široki Brijeg and Čitluk in the west, to Žitomislić in the south. Communities that lived on the territory of Herzegovina during the Iron Age, in the last centuries of the old era were exposed to various influences of their civilizationally highly developed neighbors. Indigenous communities that occupied parts of Mostar and its surroundings at the time of the arrival of the Romans, are recognized by the names Naresi and Daorsi.

The settlement culture of the new conquerors, in its narrowest concept, carried the dominant elements of Roman permanence, from social and political, to spiritual identification. The expansion of Roman land ownership was the impetus for the settlement of Italics, especially in the area around the Neretva River. Although there was a certain tolerance of the Romans towards the religions and cults of the conquered countries, various interferences through the emergence of the equalization of domestic and Roman deities (interpretatio Romana), and the penetration of Greek and Oriental cults, record a different outcome. Among the significant realizations of such a concept are monuments dedicated to the deities Jupiter (Cim, Mostar), Juno (Greeks, Streams) and the Greek god Asclepius (Hodbina, Bišće Polje). In late antiquity, the northern parts of present-day Mostar were one of the few areas where the cult of the Persian god Mithras was revered, as confirmed by a monument found in Potoci. Shortly after the conquest of the territory of today's Bosnia and Herzegovina, the Romans built major roads whose main goal was the military, political and economic connection of the conquered countries with Italy and Rome. According to archaeological finds that have been discovered but not systematically explored, the wider area of ​​Mostar was quite populated in Roman times. The 1988 census lists 39 sites in the Mostar area, but there are currently 35 sites within the Mostar municipality. Archaeological remains from Roman times indicate a dense network of settlements that were characterized by good population and economic development, and their survival was based on raising livestock for the market.

Medieval times

A new period arose with the fall of the Roman Empire and the settlement of the Slavs. During the early Middle Ages, the area of Mostar belonged to the province of Zahumlje (Hum). Despite the supreme authority of the Franks, these areas had a certain degree of independence, especially the time for Prince Mihajlo Višević (910 - 950). The area was ruled for a time by the Nemanjićs, then by the Bosnian ban, and during the 14th - 15th centuries. century, the princes of Hum are getting stronger. One of them - Stjepan Vukčić Kosača, who ruled in Blagaj near Mostar in 1448, received the title of duke (hence the name Herzegovina).

The city of Mostar, although located in a very convenient place, where the river Neretva flows out of its canyon, did not appear at all until the fifties of the XV century. In fact, Mostar does not belong to the history of medieval settlements. That is why the case of the capital Hum, Blagaj, is especially interesting. A city with the strongest and oldest tradition in this area. Its ruins can still be seen today on the karst above the source of the river Buna and today's settlement Blagaj. In the town was the court of the Hum nobleman with two cisterns, a treasury and a dungeon.

Bosnian medieval towns usually consisted of two parts, a fortress and a suburb. The fortress (castum) was the center of nobles, military crews and city administration, while merchants and craftsmen lived in the suburbs, (suburbium) where economic life developed and took place. The town of Blagaj is often mentioned in the context of the political center because after the 10th century Blagaj played a major role in the history of Hum and was mentioned as the center of the turbulent political life of medieval Bosnia. Bosnian rulers have been issuing charters here since the time of Tvrtko, and in the first half of the 15th century Blagaj became an important stronghold of the Hranić-Kosač family. Sandalj Hranić and Stjepan Vukčić mostly stayed in this city.

The land of Hum was also inhabited by King Stjepan II Kotromanić and other royal families. Under Blagaj in 1463, an agreement was reached on the demarcation and mutual agreement of Stjepan Vukčić and one of the prominent military leaders, Sultan Mehmed II. According to this agreement, all countries north and northeast of Blagaj had to belong to the Ottoman government. Blagaj was captured by the Turks in 1465, after which the center of city life, with the arrival of the Turks, moved to Mostar.

The first written mention of Mostar is also related to these two towers: the Dubrovnik report of 3 April 1452 describes how Vladislav Hercegović rebelled against his father Stjepan, stealing some of his property. The report states: "ha preso quello di ... Blagay et do castelli al ponte de Neretva (he took ... Blagaj and two fortifications on the bridge on the Neretva). As early as 1444, the bridge on the site of today's Mostar is indirectly mentioned. in the charter of the Aragonese-Neapolitan king Alfonso the Generous.

The name Mostar first appears in the Ottoman census from 1468-1469, and that it refers to the settlement around the two towers on the Neretva is clear from a document from the session of the Council of the Republic of Dubrovnik in 1474, which classifies it as a relatively older settlement in Herzegovina.

Older than Mostar are Blagaj (1297) and Gacko (1176), which is first mentioned as the main center of trade routes, with developed crafts and trade. It represented the main center of Herzegovina, Bosnia and Zeta. Today, the visible remains of the Middle Ages in Mostar and its surroundings are the walls of medieval towns and towers, as well as tombstones (stećak tombstones). The stećak tombstones are located in all parts of the municipality of Mostar, which testifies to the continuity of life in the entire municipality throughout history.

Ottoman times

After Bosnia fell to the Ottomans in 1463, a new era began in the area of today's Mostar and its surroundings. The Ottomans appropriated Blagaj even before 1466, and the former seat of the Hum rulers changed its owners. Nevertheless, Blagaj is gradually losing its role as an administrative center moving north to a town that is slowly beginning to emerge on the banks of the Neretva. At the transition from XV to XVI. century begins the history of the city on the banks of the Neretva as a new administrative and political center of the Herzegovina region.

The connection of the coasts into a single city unit was completed after the arrival of Ottoman troops, led by Sandzak-beg Isa-beg Ishakovic, in the time of Sultan Mehmed II, occupied Mostar between 1466 and 1468, and since 1469 the present name of the city has been in use. together with the Turkish name Köprű Hisar (fortress on the bridge). Evliya lebelebi, a famous travel writer from the 17th century, passing through the countries of the Ottoman Empire, visited Mostar and noted several interesting things, touching, among other things, the watchtower: “Sulejman han built very hard by the river Neretva, on stone cliffs this fortress to protects the city. To the right of the cliffs is a rather small fortress, one to the left, and among them is the world-famous Mostar Bridge. The bridge that Evliya leelebi mentions in her Travelogues is the famous Old Bridge that was completed in 1566.

Immediately on the eve of the formal fall of the Kingdom of Bosnia, the Ottoman authorities began the gradual implementation of a new administrative-territorial division in Bosnia and Herzegovina. The conquest of Bosnia meant for Sultan Mehmed II. Fatiha providing an important strategic stronghold in clashes with the Hungarian army and the Venetian Republic. They turned the conquered territories of the Ottomans into administrative units of the vilayets, which would soon be united into the Bosnian Sandzak, first mentioned by the biographer of Sultan Mehmed II, Dursun-beg in 1463. The Ottomans continued their military actions, and by 1470 a single administrative unit. The Herzegovinian sandžak is first mentioned in sources at the end of February 1470, and Hamza-beg is mentioned as its first administrator.

Bosnia and Herzegovina was part of the Ottoman Empire for more than four centuries. In that period, Mostar profiled itself as the administrative and economic center of the region, the rest of which until the second half of the XIX. century remained rural. In Mostar, despite the constant military conflicts in which the Ottoman government participated and despite the constant threat due to multiple attempts by Venetian troops to penetrate the city, the continuity of economic and cultural development was maintained. The favorable geostrategic position of the city conditioned the intensive development in the Ottoman era. Mostar became the urban and economic center of the area, rising in a very short period from the status of a hut (fortress) to the status of a town in the sense of a city settlement. The establishment of Mostar as an urban settlement began during the time of the Sandzakbeg Sinan-pasha Borovinić.

In 1506, Sinan-pasha built a mosque outside the city walls, a square, a bazaar was formed, and a strong economic and any other development began, and Mostar became a city in the true sense of the word. In the middle of the XVII. century Mostar acquires the status of a sheher (the largest type of urban environment). Trade relations with Dubrovnik, and later with Metković, developed. Mostar's traders in foreign markets are becoming competitive mainly due to the export of leather, wool, wax, tallow, honey and livestock. The more than four-century-long Ottoman rule defined Mostar as a type of urban settlement of primarily oriental Mediterranean physiognomy with elements of the western Mediterranean as well.

The bazaars functioned as a kind of lifeblood of city life and a place of intensive economic and service activity. Mostar killed them in the XVI. century had eight of which four (Velika tepa, Hendek, Kujundžiluk and Priječka čaršija) were within the city itself, and four (Pod Gradom, Gornja čaršija, Donja čaršija and Mala tepa) in the suburbs. In 1631 the city was divided into 24 mahalas of which 22 were formed around mosques. At the end of XVII. century Mostar reached the peak of its territorial development in the status of the Ottoman Sheher. Thus, the number of residential areas by the beginning of the XVIII. century to more than 30. The word mahala originally means "place", "location", or "neighborhood" and mahala as a territorial area, with 30 to 40 housing units, usually includes mekteb (school), harem (cemetery) and, of course, the mosque.

As pointed out earlier, in 1566, the Ottomans erected new stones about 20 meters from the chain bridge, which is certainly the most representative indicator of the aforementioned prosperity. Turkish sources mention another stone bridge on the river Radobolja, which flows along the western side of the city. Considering that the city infrastructure, the system of mahalas and čaršija, developed more intensively on the left bank, certain sources state assumptions about the concentration of one part of craft and trade facilities on the west bank of the Neretva. In 1639, due to numerous court disputes and complaints of locals against each other due to cases of deterrence and theft of water, Mostar received the first city water supply system, which was supplied from the river Radobolja. Of the architectural realizations relevant for defining the urban landscape in the field of sacral buildings, the most common are certainly the mosques in Mostar, which were built during the Ottoman rule of the city more than thirty. They were named after their founders and builders. In addition to the most representative, Karađoz-beg's built in 1557/58, it is certainly worth mentioning Ćejvan-ćehajina, Koski Mehmed-paša and the mosque of Nesuh-aga Vučjaković. The corpus of oriental architecture, which, in addition to mosques, adorns the streets and alleys of Mostar's old town, includes school buildings (mektebs and madrasas), water supply facilities, catering facilities (inns), residential buildings and various types of memorial buildings such as turbets. At the end of the Ottoman rule, an Orthodox cathedral was built in Mostar, as well as a Catholic (Franciscan) one.

Gymnasium

In Mostar there are many buildings built during the Austro-Hungarian period and represent a good blend of Western and Oriental architecture (pseudo-Moorish style). The most interesting are the Old Gymnasium (1898), the most elite school in the city (located on the Spanish Steps) and the City Spa (1914), designed by architect Rudolf Tonnies with the support of Miroslav Loosa. These are powerful buildings in the Austro-Hungarian style with Moorish ornaments.

During the last war the spa building was destroyed but soon after it was rebuilt.

Austro-Hungarian rule

One of the key items for considering urban changes, which occurred in all regional centers of Bosnia and Herzegovina and in Mostar after the establishment of the Austro-Hungarian protectorate in 1878, is the way of resolving the relationship between the elements of oriental provenance and new urban characteristics of Central European urban settlements. After the establishment of the Austro-Hungarian protectorate, a large number of inhabitants with completely different living habits immigrated to the territory of Bosnia and Herzegovina from those whose way of life was associated with urban settlements of oriental physiognomy.

The European way of life was primarily reflected in the segment of residential architecture and the construction of villas with gardens in accordance with the then Central European trends, primarily in parts of cities that were not built before, where the urban basis of new housing was defined by spatial planning then current Central European standards. In the field of construction momentum in public urban areas, the key indicator of a new way of life and a different structure of the population is certainly the difference in the representation of buildings for certain purposes as key to the definition of the urban environment.

The treatment of urban spaces in this period, among other things, reflects the attitude of the Austro-Hungarian authorities towards the heritage of earlier epochs and the existing urban models in Mostar. The Great War

The Austro-Hungarian occupation of Bosnia and Herzegovina in 1878 opened a new chapter in the centuries-old history of Mostar. During the forty-year rule of the Dual Monarchy, the city significantly changed the image of the urban settlement of oriental-Islamic type, and confirmed the previously acquired status of political, cultural, economic and administrative center of the region, but also one of the most important factors in the very complex BiH political scene. Mostar was given great strategic importance in Austro-Hungarian military circles. Therefore, immediately after the occupation, the existing military facilities were adjusted and new military facilities were built and equipped.1 In Mostar, three larger military complexes were built in this period (North, South with the garrison hospital and the Western camp) and the Konak building was renovated. Also in 1913, south of the city zone, a military airfield (Flugfeld) was built and an air fleet (Flugpark 4) was established, as one of a total of ten organizational and tactical formations of this type, as many as existed in the area of the Monarchy. was surrounded by a series of smaller fortifications ("fortica"), linked to a single defense system. A regiment of mountain artillery (k.u.k. Gebirgsartillerieregiment No. 7) was stationed in the area of the city, which had four batteries of cannons and 2 howitzer batteries, 3 crew and, if necessary, operational troops.

The tragic consequences of the assassination of the Austro-Hungarian heir to the throne, Franz Ferdinand, by Gavrilo Princip on June 28, 1914 in Sarajevo, were also strongly felt in the Mostar area. Demonstrations began in the city the next day, during which several Serbian shops were demolished. On July 1, 1914, the head of the Provincial Government for Bosnia and Herzegovina, Oskar Potiorek, issued an order to extend the court, which was established for the city and district of Sarajevo, to the entire country. The Court of First Instance for the city of Mostar was officially announced a day later at 11.30 am.

The heated situation in Bosnia and Herzegovina was fully marked by intensive preparations for the outbreak of war. The majority of the forces of the Austro-Hungarian VI Army, which included the XV and XVI Corps with 6 infantry divisions, gathered in two groups near Sarajevo and Mostar. The air parks, meanwhile, were reorganized into flying companies (Fliegerkompagnie, abbreviated Flik). Mostar was designated as a mobilization site for two pilots companies (Flick 4 and Flick 6), 8 which, after their establishment, were sent to operational tasks closer to the front lines. Mostar was also home to one of the most important Austro-Hungarian aviator training centers, which operated as part of the Fliegerersatzkompagnie-Flek No. 11, as well as an aircraft repair workshop (Fliegerwerkstatt). The war operations of the Montenegrin army in eastern Herzegovina, 9 and the increasingly certain entry of Italy into the war against the Central Powers, hastened the decision of the Austro-Hungarian military authorities to take measures to evacuate Mostar, which was intended as a fortified defense. The evacuation, which began on May 21, 1915, lasted until the end of November of the same year.10 During this period, the population was forbidden to return to the city, which further affected its impoverishment, and the destruction or damage of abandoned property. The immediate danger of war in the area of Mostar and Herzegovina ended with the capitulation of Montenegro on January 25, 1916. Moving the front lines, however, did not mean relief for the afflicted citizens, whose suffering continued in the following years. The war hit the population of Mostar with all its cruelty. The already difficult food supply situation was further aggravated by a catastrophic drought for several months, which hit these areas in 1917. In the last years of the war, various plant diseases appeared, such as phylloxera. This caused the decline of vineyards in the city, which were both a source of food for the local population and one of the most important economic activities. During this period, more deaths than exhaustion and hunger were recorded among the citizens.

During World War I, 1,809 soldiers were mobilized from the city to military units. Kingdom of Yugoslavia

With the capitulation of Austro-Hungary and the unification, that is, the proclamation of the Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes. In December 1918, BiH became part of a single Yugoslav state, but for some time retained some elements and forms of its statehood. At the same time, Mostar remained one of the largest cities in the Kingdom of Yugoslavia. With the combination of Oriental and modern European architecture and the harmony of modern and old, it became an uncut pearl of the Kingdom of Yugoslavia.

Mostar soon became one of the main targets of Greater Serbian politics, and violence against Bosniaks (Muslim population) was carried out in a targeted and wise manner. The coup and the arrival of the "Serbian government" in Mostar started a call for looting, seizure of land, persecution and liquidation of Muslims. The position of Muslims became unbearable, especially in the larger cities of Bosnia and Herzegovina, where Bosniaks predominated, and reports of them penetrated abroad. Thus, during his stay in Sarajevo in February 1919, the French journalist Charles Rivet talked, among others, with Reisul-ulema Caušević, and he published that conversation in the Paris newspaper Le temps. April of the same year. Presenting information about the suffering of Muslims "during the solemn creation of Yugoslavia", which they "were preparing to serve with their soul". The period of the Kingdom of Yugoslavia found an already quite modern European city, with a long tradition. In that period, 1918-1941, very little was built, so that period in the development of the city does not represent a significant period. In economic terms, nothing has been done, Mostar has failed to build any factories. Several cultural and artistic societies were formed on a national or religious basis. In the period from 1922 to 1929, Mostar was part of the Mostar region, and then the Primorska banovina, until 1939. In 1926, the workers managed to establish the cultural and artistic society Abrašević, which reached its peak during the second Yugoslavia. Preserving the tradition, it achieved notable results. From 1939 to 1941, Mostar was part of the Banovina of Croatia.

After the Axis attack on the Kingdom of Yugoslavia and the proclamation of the Independent State of Croatia, Mostar found itself in the Italian zone of interest in the territory of the Independent State of Croatia. Mostar was the headquarters of several Ustasha, Italian and German divisions and Home Guard regiments, and there was also a "Mostar Battalion" in the partisans. In the Second World War, Mostar gave a lot of young lives, who fought for one side or the other. Numerous crimes were committed by Chetniks against the Croat and Bosniak population in the Mostar area. On February 14, 1945, which is celebrated as the day of the liberation of Mostar in the Second World War, the partisans, entering Mostar, terrorized the Croatian population. Every year, the Croats of Mostar still remember the innocently killed Franciscans and other victims of the Second World War. The period of the Second Yugoslavia left a big mark on Mostar. This period, along with the Austro-Hungarian period, represents the most fruitful period in the construction, urbanization and expansion of the city of Mostar in its entire history.
